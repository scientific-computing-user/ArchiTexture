\begin{abstract}
Does frozen SAM contain texture understanding even though it was built mainly for semantic segmentation? Recent fine-tuning results suggest that texture-relevant structure may already exist inside the frozen model. We investigate that possibility through two complementary routes. Feature-space exploration asks what is already latent in frozen multiscale features; proposal-/mask-space exploration asks what is already recoverable from the generated mask bank without retraining the backbone or proposal generator. We study both routes on benchmark settings that define the current state of the art for real-world and synthesized texture segmentation, then broaden each side with a complement of its own: the natural side with a curated ADE20K subset of textural image crops, and the synthetic side with a stitched bridge benchmark that preserves exact labels while making transitions more naturalistic. The feature route reveals that coarse frozen features already support plausible texture partitions and that positional debiasing such as flip-averaging materially affects what those features reveal. The proposal route shows that generated masks also contain substantial recoverable texture structure: it outperforms a public TextureSAM rerun on both the standard real-world and synthesized benchmarks, and the same reading carries to the broader natural-image and stitched-synthetic complements. Together, the two routes indicate that frozen SAM contains texture-relevant structure in both representation and generated-mask space, while the broadened benchmark set shows that this recoverability story is not confined to one historical natural/synthetic pair alone. \codelinksentence
\end{abstract}
